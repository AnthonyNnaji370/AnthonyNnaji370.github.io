Optimal Computed Torque Control of a 3-DOF Manipulator via Genetic Algorithm

For this project, I replaced the usual trial-and-error PD gain tuning with a Genetic Algorithm that automatically searches the gain space to minimize trajectory tracking error. The difference in performance was significant.

MATLAB / Simulink Computed Torque Control Genetic Algorithm Nonlinear Dynamics PD Gain Optimization Ontario Tech University
The Problem

Computed Torque Control is powerful, but only with the right gains

Computed Torque Control (CTC) is one of the most effective nonlinear control strategies for robotic manipulators. It works by cancelling the robot's nonlinear dynamics and reducing the system to a simple linear form. But there's a catch: its performance is entirely dependent on the choice of proportional (Kp) and derivative (Kd) gains.

Pick the wrong gains and you get overshoot, oscillation, slow convergence, or outright instability. The classic approach of manual trial-and-error is tedious, does not scale well to multi-joint systems, and has no guarantee of reaching a global optimum. For a 3-DOF manipulator with nonlinear coupling between joints, the problem is especially difficult. Gains that work well in one region of the workspace may perform poorly in another.

The Approach

Letting evolution find what trial-and-error couldn't

I framed the gain tuning as an optimization problem and handed it to a Genetic Algorithm. The GA maintains a population of candidate gain sets, evaluates each one by simulating the full nonlinear manipulator model, and evolves better solutions over generations without needing gradient information or convexity assumptions.

I designed the cost function to penalize cumulative tracking error across the full trajectory, covering both position and velocity deviation. A penalty term also discouraged excessively large gains that would cause actuator saturation in a real system. That made the optimization physically meaningful, not just numerically convenient.

The GA ran with a population of 30 over 40 generations using tournament selection, scattered crossover, and adaptive Gaussian mutation. The full nonlinear Simulink model, including forward dynamics, inverse dynamics, trajectory generation, and PD-augmented CTC, was evaluated for every candidate in every generation.

Baseline Performance

What manually tuned gains actually look like

Before running the GA, I tested the controller with manually chosen gains of Kp = 50, Kd = 10. The system was stable, but the tracking was noticeably poor. Joint 1 showed the largest phase delay, Joint 2 had early overshoot, and Joint 3 deviated during mid-trajectory transitions. Velocity plots showed oscillatory, underdamped behaviour across the first few seconds of motion.

These results are typical of manual tuning in multi-joint systems. You can get close, but nonlinear coupling means improving one joint often comes at the expense of another.

Results

The GA found what manual tuning couldn't

After 40 generations, the GA converged on optimal gains of Kp = 11.5087, Kd = 10.0112, achieving a minimum cost of J = 0.0449. The optimized controller delivered tighter trajectory tracking, reduced overshoot, faster convergence, and smaller steady-state errors across all three joints.

Key Takeaways

Why this approach matters beyond the simulation

The GA framework I built here is inherently scalable. The same optimization pipeline could be applied to different trajectories, varying payloads, or uncertain model parameters, making it a practical foundation for real deployment. Unlike gradient-based methods, it imposes no convexity requirements and handles the nonlinear coupling that makes multi-joint systems so difficult to tune by hand.

What this project showed me is that evolutionary optimization and model-based control are genuinely complementary. CTC handles the physics, the GA handles the tuning, and together they produce a controller that is both analytically grounded and empirically validated.
