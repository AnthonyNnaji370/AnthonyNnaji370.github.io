Master's Research Project

Robust Trajectory Tracking of a Two-Link Manipulator

For this project, I wanted to know what actually happens when a robot arm faces real-world chaos: vibrations, external forces, unpredictable loads. I compared a classical PID controller against a hybrid Boundary-Layer Sliding Mode + PID strategy to see which one holds up under pressure.

MATLAB / Simulink PID Control Sliding Mode Control Nonlinear Dynamics Disturbance Rejection Ontario Tech University
The Problem

Robotic arms are tricky to control, especially under disturbance

A two-link robotic manipulator sounds simple enough in theory: move joint 1, move joint 2, reach the target. But once I got into the dynamics, it became clear how nonlinear, coupled, and sensitive the system really is. What happens at one joint affects the other, and any external disturbance like vibration, a sudden load, or friction can throw the whole trajectory off.

PID controllers are reliable and well-understood under ideal conditions. But once I introduced a step disturbance mid-motion, PID struggled to recover quickly. That is the gap I wanted to address in this project.

Fig. 1 — Two-link manipulator geometry and the controller equations governing both PID and Sliding Mode Control strategies.
The Approach

Combining two controllers into one robust strategy

My approach was to take PID's smooth tracking performance and add a Sliding Mode Control (SMC) term on top of it. The SMC kicks in hard when the system deviates from the desired trajectory, so together they cover each other's weaknesses.

One important design decision I made was using a boundary-layer saturation function instead of the standard sign function in SMC. The sign function causes chattering, which are rapid oscillations that stress actuators and cause real problems on hardware. The saturation function smooths that out and keeps the controller robust without that downside.

I built both controllers from scratch in MATLAB/Simulink, with separate subsystems for each joint and the full nonlinear rigid-body dynamics modeled throughout.

Fig. 2 — Simulink implementation of the PID controller for both links of the manipulator.
The Results

The numbers tell a clear story

I tested both controllers across three scenarios: nominal conditions with no disturbance, a step disturbance applied as a sudden torque jolt at t = 5s, and a sinusoidal disturbance as a continuous oscillating force. The hybrid controller came out on top in all three.

The step disturbance test was the most telling one for me. The hybrid controller recovered nearly instantly while PID kept oscillating well past the disturbance window. Post-disturbance RMSE for PID+SMC on Link 1 was 0.004403 compared to 0.005174 for PID. The absolute numbers are small, but the speed of recovery is where the real difference shows up.

Fig. 4 — Step disturbance applied at t = 5s. PID+SMC (red) snaps back to reference trajectory immediately. Classical PID (blue) takes significantly longer to recover.
Takeaways

What this project actually taught me

The headline result, that the hybrid controller is better, did not surprise me. What I found more interesting was the trade-off: PID+SMC consistently used more control effort to get its performance gains. That is the real engineering question in any application. Is the precision gain worth the actuator cost? For precision manufacturing or surgical robotics, yes. For a simple pick-and-place task, probably not.

This project also reinforced for me how much implementation matters. Getting MATLAB/Simulink to behave with coupled nonlinear dynamics, proper disturbance injection timing, and clean performance metric calculation across three scenarios was where most of the real work happened. Deriving the equations was the easy part.
